The gradient of $kT$ in the flux term on each of the surfaces in cell $(i,j,k)$ is computing using formulations similar to Equation \ref{eqn:ellipsoidal_approx_grad}. Then the flux integral is simply the sum over all
surfaces. Since the gradient on any surface only involves the cell of interest and its neighbor across that surface, the flux term only communicates with up to 6 neighboring cells.

\begin{equation}
    \oiint_{\partial V_{ijk}} \nabla kT \cdot \mathbf{\hat{n}} dS \approx \sum_{s=1}^{N_{\text{surf}}} \left[ \nabla kT \cdot \mathbf{\hat{n}} \right]_{ijk,s}  S_{ijk,s}.
    \label{eqn:heat_interior_flux}
\end{equation}

Equation \ref{eqn:heat_interior_flux} works well for interior cells. Boundary cells typically have the flux terms for its boundary surfaces modified to account for boundary conditions. Six boundary conditions are
required to fully describe the system; however, if the computing domain is a whole ellipsoid, five of them do not need to be specified.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The flux terms on the surfaces $r = 0$ and $\mu = \pm 1$ are readily omitted. This can be justified by the degenerate surface that collapses at those points.
    \item The surfaces $\varphi = -\pi$ and $\varphi = \pi$ form a set of periodic boundaries. 
\end{enumerate}

The only boundary condition that needs specifying is that at $r = 1$, which can be classified as either Dirichlet, Neumann, or Robin. In the following formulation of each boundary condition, function evaluations are
almost always done at surface centroid of a boundary cell, i.e. at ($\mu_j, \varphi_k$), so the angular dependencies and the associated subscripts are implicit unless noted otherwise.

\subsection{Dirichlet boundary condition} 
The boundary condition takes the form
\begin{equation}
    T(1) = T^b.
\end{equation}

A layer of ghost cells can be constructed that extends to $r_{N_r + 3/2}$ and perscribed a temperature that averages out to $T^b$ when combined with the temperature at cell $N_r$. The average is just the arthimetic
mean of the two temperatures if the ghost cell and its neighboring boundary cell are equivolume. The boundary of such a ghost cell is then
\begin{subequations}
    \begin{align}
        r_{N_r + 3/2}^3 - 1 &= 1 - r_{N_r - 1/2}^3 \\
        r_{N_r + 3/2}^3 &= \sqrt[3]{2 - r_{N_r - 1/2}^3} 
    \end{align}
\end{subequations}

The partial derivative with respect to $r$ at $r = 1$ is calculated as the central difference between the temperatures in the ghost and boundary cells
\begin{subequations}
    \label{eqn:heat_dirichlet}
    \begin{equation}
        \left. \frac{\partial kT}{\partial r} \right|_{N_r+1/2} = \frac{k(T^g)T^g - k(T_{N_r})T_{N_r}}{\frac{1}{2} \sqrt[3]{2 - r_{N_r - 1/2}^3} - r_{N_r - 1/2}},
        \label{eqn:heat_dirichlet}
    \end{equation}
    where
    \begin{equation}
        T^g \equiv 2T^b - T_{N_r}
    \end{equation}
\end{subequations}
is the ghost cell temperature.

\subsection{Neumann boundary condition}
The boundary condition takes the form
\begin{equation}
    k \nabla T (1) \cdot \mathbf{\hat{n}}_r(1) = f,
\end{equation}

which directly specifies the flux on the outer surface.

\subsection{Robin boundary condition}
The boundary condition takes the form
\begin{equation}
    a T^b + b\nabla T^b \cdot \mathbf{\hat{n}}_r^b +  = f.
\end{equation}

There are 4 total unknowns -- one in $T^b$ and three in $\nabla T^b$. Similar to the Dirichlet case, $T^b$ is converted into the ghost cell temperature $T^g$:
\begin{equation}
    a \frac{T^g + T_{N_r}}{2} + b\nabla T^b \cdot \mathbf{\hat{n}}_r^b +  = f.
\end{equation}

This allows us to write the temperature derivative in terms of $T^g$ the same way as Equation \ref{eqn:heat_dirichlet}. Substituting the derivative approximation into Equation \ref{eqn:ellipsoidal_approx_grad}
supplies the remaining three equations needed to solve for $T^g$ and $\nabla T^b$.
\begin{equation}
    \nabla T^b = \mathbf{J} \mathbf{G}^{-1}
    \begin{bmatrix}
        \displaystyle \frac{ T^g - T_{N_r} }{ \frac{1}{2} \sqrt[3]{2 - r_{N_r - 1/2}^3} - r_{N_r - 1/2} } \\
        \displaystyle \frac{ \nabla T^b \cdot \Delta_j \mathbf{r}_{i+1/2,k} }{\Delta \mu_j} \\
        \displaystyle \frac{ \nabla T^b \cdot \Delta_k \mathbf{r}_{i+1/2,j} }{\Delta \varphi_k}
    \end{bmatrix},
    \label{eqn:eqn:heat_robin_gradTb}
\end{equation}
